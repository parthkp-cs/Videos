# Episode 1: "How to Steal an Island" — The Origins (874 AD – 1262)
## Full Production Script — Iceland Series, Episode 1 of 3

**Target runtime:** 12–15 minutes
**Narration pace:** ~140 wpm | Narration word count: ~1,750 words
**Color palette:** Warm amber and burnt sienna for the Viking/Settlement age; muted greens and stone grey for the Commonwealth era; deep red accents at the Sturlung Age

## PART ONE: BEFORE THE VIKINGS, THERE WERE THE MONKS

Actually, back up slightly further.

**[VISUAL: The year counter ticks back a bit further — no specific year, just a blurred "c.800s." The map of Iceland reappears, completely empty. Not even a label. Just coastline.]**

Because Iceland didn't start with Vikings. It started with monks.

**[VISUAL: IRISH PAPAR MONK appears — a small, robed figure with a tonsure haircut, sandals, and an expression of profound contentment. He's sitting cross-legged on a volcanic rock, eyes closed, holding a small bell. He looks like a man who has achieved exactly what he set out to achieve. Around him: nothing. Perfect nothing.]**

Before any Norse settler set foot on the island, a small group of Irish Christian hermits — the papar, as the old texts call them — had already been living there. They had sailed north deliberately, looking for the most remote, God-soaked silence they could find, somewhere between the known world and the edge of it.

**[VISUAL: A tiny boat on a vast grey sea. The boat has one monk in it. He is rowing steadily. A small label appears on the sea: "The Known World" with an arrow pointing back left. Another label points forward right: "Probably Fine."]**

They found Iceland. They settled in. They were having a perfectly good solitary existence.

**[VISUAL: The monk, now on land, looking serenely happy. Birds. Steam from hot springs. A small cross planted in the ground. His expression: pure peace.]**

Then the Norse arrived, and the monks packed up their bells and their books and their croziers, and left.

**[VISUAL: joke — A longship appears on the horizon. Cut back to the monk. His expression shifts from serene to deeply, quietly resigned — not afraid, just: "of course." He begins methodically packing a small bag. Bell. Book. Crozier. He lifts his bag and walks left, off screen, while the longships multiply on the right. He does not look back.]**

They had come all this way specifically to be alone. Iceland's very first residents chose it for the same reason its founders would: to get away from people with opinions about how they should live.

**[VISUAL: hold — The monks' tiny cross still stands on the empty shore as the longships approach. Hold one beat. Then the camera moves.]**

## PART TWO: THE FOUNDING — FURNITURE DELIVERY LOGISTICS

**[VISUAL: The amber-toned map of Iceland, with Norway visible to the east. A ship leaves Norway's coast. A small figure stands at the prow.]**

**[VISUAL: INGÓLFR ARNARSON appears — tall, weathered, wearing a fur-collared cloak. He's standing at the prow of his ship looking determined. His expression is the particular stubbornness of a man who has made a decision he cannot un-make.]**

The man credited with the first permanent Norse settlement was Ingólfr Arnarson, who arrived in 874 AD. He was fleeing Norway — King Harald Fairhair was busy consolidating power back home, which is a polite way of saying he was making life unpleasant for anyone who disagreed with him. Ingólfr didn't disagree politely. He left.

**[VISUAL: King Harald Fairhair in the background of Norway — large, self-satisfied, wearing an extremely elaborate crown. His hair is very impressive. A small label appears near his head: "Harald Fairhair (consolidating power)." He looks pleased with himself. A tiny Ingólfr walks away from the scene in the opposite direction with his bags.]**

His method for choosing where to build his home is one of history's great examples of logistical commitment.

**[VISUAL: joke — A map of Iceland's coastline. Then a dotted sailing route that goes around... and around... and around the island. Duration label appears: "3 years." The route has little notes along it: "not here," "still not here," "checked this bit already." The mood is patient but increasingly baffled.]**

He threw his high-seat pillars overboard — these were ornately carved wooden posts, central to Norse household and religious life — and pledged to settle wherever they washed ashore.

**[VISUAL: Ingólfr's hands releasing two large carved wooden pillars into the sea. They bob in the waves. He watches them go. His expression is resolute. The pillars float away. He continues to watch. The pillars are still floating. He watches some more.]**

Then he spent three years sailing along the coast looking for them.

**[VISUAL: joke — A "MISSING" notice, styled like a Norse-era version of a lost-and-found poster. At the top: a sketch of the two carved pillars. Description: "Ornately carved. Tall. Very important. Last seen: overboard." Reward: "A capital city."]**

They eventually turned up at a bay where steam rose from hot springs in the ground.

**[VISUAL: The two pillars, wedged in a rocky bay, steam rising gently behind them. They look like they've been waiting. Ingólfr arrives by boat. His expression: relief, mixed with slight embarrassment at how long that took.]**

He named it Reykjavík. Smoky Bay. Iceland's capital was chosen by furniture delivery logistics, and it has been the capital ever since.

**[VISUAL: A small animated city label fades in: "REYKJAVÍK — Capital of Iceland (est. 874, via maritime logistics)." The two pillars now stand in the foreground. Birds perch on them. It looks settled. Official. Inevitable.]**

## PART THREE: THE GREAT MIGRATION

Between 874 and 930, somewhere between eight thousand and twenty thousand settlers followed.

**[VISUAL: The Iceland map fills slowly with small human figures — trickling in from the right (Norway) and also from the south (the British Isles). They spread across the island like water finding its level. A population counter ticks up in the corner: 1,000... 5,000... 8,000–20,000. It stops with a small note: "(the records aren't certain)." The note is factual but somehow charming.]**

Most came from western Norway. Some came from Norse-settled communities in Britain and Ireland, bringing Irish wives and Irish slaves whose DNA is still visible in Icelanders today.

**[VISUAL: The incoming settlers arrive from two directions. Arrows from Norway to the right. Arrows from the British Isles below. The Iceland map shows the island slowly filling up. A small annotation appears: "Genetic evidence, 1,000+ years later." The note is presented with quiet weight.]**

They came because Norway was overcrowded, because Harald was king, and because some people would sail into an active volcanic field before they'd submit to a monarch.

**[VISUAL: joke — A volcanic fissure erupting on the left side of the map. On the right side: Norway with King Harald's crown sitting on top of it. A row of small settler figures are visibly choosing the volcano. They have not hesitated.]**

Iceland was, from its very first days, a refuge for people who did not want to be governed.

**[VISUAL: hold — The island, now dotted with settler figures, sits in the dark Atlantic. The light on screen is warm amber. No labels. Just people on an island, far from anything. Music: low, quiet Icelandic strings. Hold for several beats.]**

Which made it all the more remarkable that they built a government anyway.

**[VISUAL: A gentle musical shift upward — not dramatic, just a little brighter. The map's amber tones warm further. A new label appears at the rift valley in the southwest: "Þingvellir."]**

## PART FOUR: THE ALTHING — A PARLIAMENT IN A RIFT VALLEY

**[VISUAL: ÞINGVELLIR — the landscape itself becomes the visual. A sweeping aerial view of the rift valley: dark basalt rock walls, flat plains, sky. Then the map simplifies back to flat illustration, showing the valley as a distinct notch in Iceland's southwest. Label: "Þingvellir — where the tectonic plates are pulling apart."]**

In 930, the Althing convened for the first time at Þingvellir — a dramatic rift valley where the North American and Eurasian tectonic plates are slowly pulling apart, as if the land itself was making a point about freedom.

**[VISUAL: joke — The two tectonic plates are shown as illustrated landmasses, slowly, visibly drifting apart. Each plate has a little label: "North America" and "Eurasia." A small caption appears at the bottom: "(approximately 2 cm per year — they're committed)." Between the plates, the Althing has set up its assembly ground right in the crack.]**

Thirty-six chieftains gathered for what was, by any serious assessment, the world's oldest surviving parliamentary institution.

**[VISUAL: Thirty-six figures — the goðar, the chieftains — file into the rift valley. Each one has a distinct silhouette. They take their positions in a rough semi-circle. No thrones. No palace. Open sky. A label appears: "930 CE — The Althing." Small secondary label: "World's oldest surviving parliament." The label glows gently, as if aware of its importance.]**

No king. No standing army. No central state. Just laws, spoken aloud from memory by the Lawspeaker over his three-year term, and a system of public dispute resolution that somehow held together a population scattered across a volcanic island with no roads.

**[VISUAL: LAWSPEAKER figure — standing on the Law Rock (Lögberg), arms slightly raised, speaking. His expression is one of calm authority. No weapons visible anywhere. The crowd listens. This is the system.]**

**[VISUAL: joke — A small organizational chart appears in the corner of the screen. At the top: "GOVERNMENT." Below it: three items connected by lines — "Laws (memorized)," "Meetings (annual, June)," and "Roads (none)." The chart is neat and official-looking. The "Roads (none)" line is there without comment.]**

It was not a democracy in any modern sense. The chieftains held the power. But it was deliberative, it was public, and it worked.

**[VISUAL: hold — The Althing assembly at Þingvellir. Wide shot. Figures talking, gesturing, debating. Mountains in the background. Sky above. Music holds quietly. Pause here before the next section.]**

## PART FIVE: THE YEAR 1000 — A DEAL UNDER A FUR CLOAK

The Althing's finest hour came around the year 1000, when Iceland faced its most consequential decision: Christianity or paganism?

**[VISUAL: The Þingvellir map splits down the middle. Left half: pagan symbols — runes, a hammer of Thor, a rough idol. Right half: a simple cross. The divide is literal and neat. A question mark hovers over the center. Music pauses.]**

Norway's King Óláfr Tryggvason was pressuring conversion. Half of Iceland was already Christian. The other half was not. Civil war was a real possibility.

**[VISUAL: King Óláfr Tryggvason in the background — visible but distant, hand extended toward Iceland in a gesture that reads as both invitation and demand. Two groups of Icelanders face each other across the divide. Neither group looks willing to move first.]**

The two factions agreed to let the lawspeaker, Þorgeir Þorkelsson, decide.

**[VISUAL: ÞORGEIR ÞORKELSSON appears — the lawspeaker. His face is intelligent and tired, like a man who knows he's been handed something impossible. He stands between the two groups. Both sides look at him. He looks at neither. He looks at the ground.]**

Þorgeir — himself a pagan — disappeared under his fur cloak for a full day and a full night. He meditated. He thought. He emerged and announced his verdict: Iceland would be Christian.

**[VISUAL: Time lapse: Þorgeir sitting cross-legged under a large fur cloak. The sun moves across the sky. Stars appear. The sun returns. The cloak does not move. Then it does. He stands. His expression has changed — it's settled now. Resolved. He speaks.]**

**[VISUAL: joke — A small "TERMS & CONDITIONS" document unfurls on screen, styled in mock-medieval script. Heading: "CHRISTIANITY — Iceland Edition." Item 1: "One God. Agreed." Item 2: "No horsemeat in public. Fine." Item 3: "Horsemeat in private. Permitted." Item 4: "Exposure of unwanted infants. Still allowed (for now)." Footer: "Negotiated by a pagan. Ratified by everyone. Nobody went to war." The document has a small seal at the bottom.]**

But — and this is the part that tells you everything about Iceland — Christianity came with a horsemeat exemption. Private consumption of horse was still permitted. Exposure of unwanted infants was still allowed. The new faith was adopted on Icelandic terms, negotiated by a pagan who understood that the alternative was bloodshed.

Pragmatism dressed up as theology. It would not be the last time Iceland repackaged necessity as principle.

**[VISUAL: Þorgeir walking away from the assembly. Behind him, the two groups are no longer separated — they've moved slightly toward each other. Nobody is happy, exactly, but nobody is fighting. A small label appears: "c.1000 CE — Compromise achieved." It's understated. Which is the point.]**

## PART SIX: THE RED, THE SON, AND THE GREATEST REAL ESTATE FRAUD IN HISTORY

Around 982, Iceland produced its most famous export of the era: a man too violent even for Iceland.

**[VISUAL: ERIK THE RED appears — broad-shouldered, red-bearded, with an expression caught between confidence and the specific energy of a man who has been asked to leave several places. He is looking at the horizon. The horizon looks concerned.]**

Erik the Red had already been exiled from Norway for manslaughter before arriving here. Iceland exiled him too — also for manslaughter.

**[VISUAL: joke — A "NOTICE OF EXILE" document. Two are shown side by side. Left: "NORWAY — Notice of Exile, issued to Eiríkr Rauði, reason: manslaughter." Right: "ICELAND — Notice of Exile, issued to Eiríkr Rauði, reason: manslaughter." Both documents have the same official stamp. At the bottom, a small annotation: "Second offence. Same offence." Erik stands between them, looking unsurprised.]**

Sensing a pattern in his reviews, Erik sailed west.

**[VISUAL: Erik on his ship, sailing west on the map. No hesitation. He has been this way before. He knows the routine now. The map scrolls west with him.]**

He found a large island, mostly covered in ice. He named it Greenland.

**[VISUAL: Greenland appears on the map, grey-white with ice. Erik stands on its coast, looking at it. A thought bubble above his head is initially blank. Then it fills with the word "Greenland." His expression: inspired. Calculated. Committed.]**

**[VISUAL: joke — A side-by-side comparison map. Left: "ICELAND" — the island is labelled, and a small inset shows it's mostly green and habitable. Label: "Named: Iceland." Right: "GREENLAND" — the island is labelled, and a small inset shows it's almost entirely ice. Label: "Named: Greenland." A small arrow between the two maps points back and forth with the label "These are backwards." The maps just sit there being accurate.]**

The Eiríks saga rauða is essentially candid about this: he chose the name specifically to attract settlers. Iceland — the island with the friendly name — has comparatively little ice on its surface. Greenland — the island with the aspirational name — is nearly buried under the largest ice sheet in the Northern Hemisphere. The naming worked. Fourteen ships of settlers followed him back. Real estate has never been the same.

**[VISUAL: Fourteen ship icons sail toward Greenland. They look hopeful. A small label: "14 ships, c.985–986 CE." Hold one beat.]**

Erik's son Leif did one better.

**[VISUAL: LEIF ERIKSSON appears — younger than his father, lighter in manner, with an expression of genuine curiosity. He's looking west from the Greenland coast, shielding his eyes. He looks like someone who considers the horizon a reasonable destination.]**

Around the year 1000 — we now know from tree-ring evidence that Norse settlers occupied L'Anse aux Meadows in Newfoundland in exactly 1021 CE — Leif Eriksson sailed west from Greenland, found an entire continent, established a settlement he called Leifsbuðir, and then went home.

**[VISUAL: Leif's ship sails west from Greenland. The map extends — Greenland, ocean, then a coastline appears with a small label: "Leifsbuðir / L'Anse aux Meadows, Newfoundland." A date counter appears: "1021 CE (tree-ring confirmed)." This is treated with quiet significance. Then: Leif's ship turns around and sails back east.]**

Five hundred years before Columbus. Leif found North America, filed no paperwork, and left.

**[VISUAL: joke — A filing cabinet labelled "DISCOVERY RECORDS." It is completely empty. A small sticky note on the drawer reads: "Leif Eriksson — checked in, checked out. No forms submitted." Next to the empty cabinet: a second drawer labelled "Columbus (1492)" — it is overflowing with paper. The drawer won't close. A ribbon is tied around it.]**

History moved on without him.

**[VISUAL: hold — The map of the North Atlantic sits quietly with Greenland and a faint outline of Newfoundland visible. Music: a single Icelandic string note, slightly mournful, slightly wry. Hold.]**

## PART SEVEN: WHILE EVERYONE ELSE WAS NOT WRITING THINGS DOWN

Back in Iceland, something else was happening that would matter far more to the world than a temporary camp in Newfoundland. Iceland was writing.

**[VISUAL: Cut back to Iceland. The color palette shifts slightly — it's the 12th and 13th centuries now; warm amber gives way to candlelight gold and muted greens. A figure at a writing desk, quill in hand, surrounded by stacked manuscripts. The desk is neat. There is a lot of material.]**

In the 12th and 13th centuries, Icelanders produced the most sophisticated prose literature of medieval Europe: the family sagas, the histories of Norse kings, and — most consequentially — the mythology.

**[VISUAL: Manuscript pages furl across the screen — not opening like a book, more like they're being laid out — each one illustrated with a different scene. A battle. A farm. A god with a hammer. A ship. They arrange themselves like a mosaic. Label: "The Sagas — 12th–13th centuries."]**

**[VISUAL: SNORRI STURLUSON appears — born 1179. He is at a writing desk, mid-thought, stylus paused over a page. He has the look of someone who is simultaneously composing something important and also aware that he is very good at it. He is not modest. He is not wrong.]**

The man who synthesised it all was Snorri Sturluson, born in 1179. His Prose Edda is, quite simply, the reason we know what we know about Norse mythology. Thor, Odin, Loki, Ragnarök — the entire cosmology that has since spawned films, comics, and theme park rides — exists in its current form because a 13th-century Icelander wrote it down.

**[VISUAL: As the gods are named, brief illustrated portraits appear — Thor with his hammer, Odin with his ravens, Loki with his sideways smile. Each is clearly styled to match the show's flat-illustration aesthetic. Then they recede and Snorri's quill takes their place.]**

**[VISUAL: joke — A modern-era logo appears: a very familiar-looking Marvel Thor. Then a small hand-drawn banner slides in front of it: "Based on a true story. Source: one Icelander, 13th century." The banner is modest. The claim is enormous.]**

Without Snorri, those gods would be as obscure as the gods of the Angles and Saxons. You're welcome, Marvel.

**[VISUAL: The illustrated Norse gods raise a collective arm in acknowledgment. Snorri, still at his desk, does not look up. He already knew.]**

Snorri was also a politician. And like most Icelandic politicians of his era, this eventually got him killed.

**[VISUAL: Snorri's expression shifts — from focused intellectual absorption to the subtle tension of someone who has started paying attention to the political situation. His quill slows. He looks up. Music shifts: something lower, slightly heavier.]**

## PART EIGHT: THE STURLUNG AGE — EVERYTHING FALLS APART

**[VISUAL: The color temperature drops. Amber recedes. The Iceland map now shows not one group of people but many, clustered in rival territories. Lines appear between them — not borders, something more fractured. Labels: "The great families." The warm glow is gone.]**

The Sturlung Age — roughly 1220 to 1262 — was Iceland's civil war.

**[VISUAL: A timeline bar appears: 1220 to 1262. It is labeled simply: "The Sturlung Age." It is lit in deep red. Below it, small event markers appear: raids, assassinations, revenge killings. There are a lot of markers.]**

The great family clans tore the island apart in a forty-year cycle of raids, assassinations, and revenge killings.

**[VISUAL: The Iceland map fragments — not literally, but its unified amber warmth is replaced by a patchwork of competing territories, each slightly different in color. Small figures move between them in the wrong direction. This is not exploration. This is not settlement. This is something else.]**

Norway's King Hákon IV watched, and waited, and quietly backed different factions in turn.

**[VISUAL: KING HÁKON IV of Norway — watching from the right edge of the map, across the sea. His expression is patient and calculating. He is not involved. Yet. A small chess piece sits in his hand. He turns it over slowly. He is in no hurry.]**

Snorri himself had become a vassal of Hákon, then fallen out of favour.

**[VISUAL: Snorri, now older than when we first met him, standing in a more exposed position on the map. The warmth around him has cooled. He is still sharp, but there is something wary in his posture now. The chess piece in Hákon's hand has changed position.]**

In September 1241, Hákon ordered him killed.

**[VISUAL: A date label: "23 September 1241." Simple. Direct. The chess piece is placed.]**

Gissur Þorvaldsson arrived at Snorri's farmhouse at Reykholt with seventy men in the middle of the night.

**[VISUAL: A farmhouse at night. Dark. Silent. Then: seventy small figures approaching across a dark field. No sound cue. No dramatic music. Just the image. The farmhouse light is on in one window.]**

**[VISUAL: GISSUR ÞORVALDSSON appears — leading the group. His expression is not triumphant. Not gleeful. He is doing a job. That, somehow, makes it worse.]**

Snorri was found hiding in his cellar.

**[VISUAL: The cellar. Low ceiling. Stone walls. Snorri crouched in the corner, holding a lamp. He looks up. This is the face of a man who has run out of moves.]**

His last recorded words were "Eigi skal höggva." Do not strike.

**[VISUAL: The words appear on screen, in the original Norse, slowly: "Eigi skal höggva." Below them, the translation: "Do not strike." No illustration. Just the words. Hold for four full seconds. Music stops entirely.]**

They struck.

**[VISUAL: hold — Blackout. Single beat of silence. Then: the sound of wind. Music returns, very low, a single note held. Fade back to the map of Iceland.]**

## PART NINE: THE OLD COVENANT — SURRENDER BY CHOICE

The Icelandic Commonwealth outlived him by barely twenty years.

**[VISUAL: The timeline bar resumes: 1241... 1250... 1262. Each year slightly heavier. The map's patchwork fragmentation gets worse.]**

By 1262, the chieftains were exhausted. The bloodshed had gone on too long, the clans were too broken, and Hákon's Norway was offering peace in exchange for submission.

**[VISUAL: The chieftains — the same figures we saw at the Althing — now look different. Fewer of them. Thinner. Tired. They stand not in a deliberative assembly but in a ragged group, looking at one another across the ruins of what the Sturlung Age left. King Hákon's chess piece is placed in the center of the map.]**

They signed the Old Covenant — the Gamli sáttmáli — and handed themselves to the Norwegian king.

**[VISUAL: A document — the Gamli sáttmáli — unfurls. Figures approach it one by one and mark it with seals. No celebration. No fanfare. This is resignation. A new flag — Norway's — rises slowly over the Iceland map. The Althing's assembly ground at Þingvellir is still visible. Still there. But quieter.]**

It was not conquest. It was surrender by choice, which is somehow more Icelandic.

**[VISUAL: joke — A scoreboard. Left column: "Conquered by force." Right column: "Chose to submit, terms negotiated, own laws preserved." A checkmark appears in the right column. The left column remains empty. The scoreboard is presented very neutrally, as if this is a perfectly normal way to evaluate a nation's history.]**

A people who had settled the edge of the world rather than accept a king had spent three centuries building something extraordinary: a parliament, a legal tradition, a literary culture, and a very specific stubbornness about doing things their own way. They handed it over when the alternative was more of their own neighbours killing each other.

**[VISUAL: Wide shot of Þingvellir — the rift valley. The tectonic plates still slowly pulling apart. The assembly ground empty now but intact. The Law Rock still standing. Light fading toward dusk. Hold this image. Let it breathe.]**

What they built in those three and a half centuries didn't disappear. It went underground.

**[VISUAL: hold — The Þingvellir plain. The camera slowly pulls back. Then: the ground itself pulses very faintly — once — as if something is still alive beneath it. Subtle. Not dramatic. Just: it's still there. Music holds a single low note.]**

## CLOSE — THE LOOP

The Althing would be abolished, restored, abolished again. Foreign kings would trade Iceland like a ledger item. Plagues would come. Famines would come. Pirates from North Africa would raid the coast.

**[VISUAL: A rapid montage — not frantic, but deliberate. Each event gets one brief image: the Althing's light going out (abolished). A ledger book with "Iceland" written as a line item. A plague ship arriving. A famine: empty shelves. A corsair ship off a rocky coast. Each image is two seconds. No music, just the narration.]**

The country would be occupied by Britain, then America. Its banks would explode. Its volcano would shut down European airspace.

**[VISUAL: The montage continues, rolling forward in time: a Union Jack planted, briefly, on Icelandic soil. Three bank buildings collapsing. An ash cloud drifting eastward across Europe. Three seconds each.]**

And through all of it, that original instinct — the one that made people sail to the edge of the world in the first place — kept surfacing. The stubbornness. The pragmatism. The willingness to do something nobody else thought was rational and simply refuse to back down.

**[VISUAL: The montage slows. Each image fades, and underneath each one — the same image from the origin story glows through: the monks walking away. Ingólfr following his pillars. The Althing at Þingvellir. Snorri at his desk. These are the roots showing through.]**

October 2008. A tiny island. Eleven times its GDP in collapsing bank assets. The IMF at the door.

**[VISUAL: Return to the cold open image: the Iceland map, small, alone in the dark North Atlantic. The three bank icons appear again on the island, toppling.]**

The foundation for what happened next was laid in 874.

**[VISUAL: hold — Wide, final shot. The island of Iceland, seen from above, amber and lit from within, in a dark sea. The tectonic plates visible on either side, still slowly, patiently pulling apart. Steam rising from the ground. The island glowing. Music: the opening Icelandic strings theme returns, wider now, resolved but not triumphant. Just: present. Still here.]**

**[VISUAL: End card fades in: "Episode 2 — The Long Dark: Plague, Monopoly, and a Man Who Ruled Iceland for Two Months" with the series logo. Hold.]**
