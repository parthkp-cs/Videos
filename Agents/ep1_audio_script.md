October 2008. Iceland's three biggest banks — Kaupthing, Landsbanki, Glitnir — collapsed in a single week. Their combined assets were eleven times the country's entire GDP. The currency lost more than half its value in days. The stock market shed over ninety percent. Grocery stores started running short on imported food. A country of three hundred thousand people was, by every measurable standard, bankrupt.

Most countries, at that point, would have done what their creditors told them to do. Bail out the banks. Repay the foreign depositors. Take the austerity package. Smile for the IMF cameras.

Iceland jailed its bankers. Told the UK and the Netherlands to get lost. Let the banks fail. And recovered faster than anyone.

To understand how a tiny island builds that kind of backbone, you have to go back to the very beginning...

...874 AD.

Actually, back up slightly further. Because Iceland didn't start with Vikings. It started with monks.

Before any Norse settler set foot on the island, a small group of Irish Christian hermits — the papar, as the old texts call them — had already been living there. They had sailed north deliberately, looking for the most remote, God-soaked silence they could find, somewhere between the known world and the edge of it. They found Iceland. They settled in. They were having a perfectly good solitary existence.

Then the Norse arrived, and the monks packed up their bells and their books and their croziers, and left. They had come all this way specifically to be alone. Iceland's very first residents chose it for the same reason its founders would: to get away from people with opinions about how they should live.

The man credited with the first permanent Norse settlement was Ingólfr Arnarson, who arrived in 874 AD. He was fleeing Norway — King Harald Fairhair was busy consolidating power back home, which is a polite way of saying he was making life unpleasant for anyone who disagreed with him. Ingólfr didn't disagree politely. He left.

His method for choosing where to build his home is one of history's great examples of logistical commitment. He threw his high-seat pillars overboard — these were ornately carved wooden posts, central to Norse household and religious life — and pledged to settle wherever they washed ashore. Then he spent three years sailing along the coast looking for them.

They eventually turned up at a bay where steam rose from hot springs in the ground. He named it Reykjavík. Smoky Bay. Iceland's capital was chosen by furniture delivery logistics, and it has been the capital ever since.

Between 874 and 930, somewhere between eight thousand and twenty thousand settlers followed. Most came from western Norway. Some came from Norse-settled communities in Britain and Ireland, bringing Irish wives and Irish slaves whose DNA is still visible in Icelanders today. They came because Norway was overcrowded, because Harald was king, and because some people would sail into an active volcanic field before they'd submit to a monarch. Iceland was, from its very first days, a refuge for people who did not want to be governed.

Which made it all the more remarkable that they built a government anyway.

In 930, the Althing convened for the first time at Þingvellir — a dramatic rift valley where the North American and Eurasian tectonic plates are slowly pulling apart, as if the land itself was making a point about freedom. Thirty-six chieftains gathered for what was, by any serious assessment, the world's oldest surviving parliamentary institution. No king. No standing army. No central state. Just laws, spoken aloud from memory by the Lawspeaker over his three-year term, and a system of public dispute resolution that somehow held together a population scattered across a volcanic island with no roads.

It was not a democracy in any modern sense. The chieftains held the power. But it was deliberative, it was public, and it worked.

The Althing's finest hour came around the year 1000, when Iceland faced its most consequential decision: Christianity or paganism? Norway's King Óláfr Tryggvason was pressuring conversion. Half of Iceland was already Christian. The other half was not. Civil war was a real possibility.

The two factions agreed to let the lawspeaker, Þorgeir Þorkelsson, decide. Þorgeir — himself a pagan — disappeared under his fur cloak for a full day and a full night. He meditated. He thought. He emerged and announced his verdict: Iceland would be Christian.

But — and this is the part that tells you everything about Iceland — Christianity came with a horsemeat exemption. Private consumption of horse was still permitted. Exposure of unwanted infants was still allowed. The new faith was adopted on Icelandic terms, negotiated by a pagan who understood that the alternative was bloodshed. Pragmatism dressed up as theology. It would not be the last time Iceland repackaged necessity as principle.

Around 982, Iceland produced its most famous export of the era: a man too violent even for Iceland. Erik the Red had already been exiled from Norway for manslaughter before arriving here. Iceland exiled him too — also for manslaughter. Sensing a pattern in his reviews, Erik sailed west.

He found a large island, mostly covered in ice. He named it Greenland. The Eiríks saga rauða is essentially candid about this: he chose the name specifically to attract settlers. Iceland — the island with the friendly name — has comparatively little ice on its surface. Greenland — the island with the aspirational name — is nearly buried under the largest ice sheet in the Northern Hemisphere. The naming worked. Fourteen ships of settlers followed him back. Real estate has never been the same.

Erik's son Leif did one better. Around the year 1000 — we now know from tree-ring evidence that Norse settlers occupied L'Anse aux Meadows in Newfoundland in exactly 1021 CE — Leif Eriksson sailed west from Greenland, found an entire continent, established a settlement he called Leifsbuðir, and then went home. Five hundred years before Columbus. Leif found North America, filed no paperwork, and left. History moved on without him.

Back in Iceland, something else was happening that would matter far more to the world than a temporary camp in Newfoundland. Iceland was writing.

In the 12th and 13th centuries, Icelanders produced the most sophisticated prose literature of medieval Europe: the family sagas, the histories of Norse kings, and — most consequentially — the mythology. The man who synthesised it all was Snorri Sturluson, born in 1179. His Prose Edda is, quite simply, the reason we know what we know about Norse mythology. Thor, Odin, Loki, Ragnarök — the entire cosmology that has since spawned films, comics, and theme park rides — exists in its current form because a 13th-century Icelander wrote it down. Without Snorri, those gods would be as obscure as the gods of the Angles and Saxons. You're welcome, Marvel.

Snorri was also a politician. And like most Icelandic politicians of his era, this eventually got him killed.

The Sturlung Age — roughly 1220 to 1262 — was Iceland's civil war. The great family clans tore the island apart in a forty-year cycle of raids, assassinations, and revenge killings. Norway's King Hákon IV watched, and waited, and quietly backed different factions in turn. Snorri himself had become a vassal of Hákon, then fallen out of favour. In September 1241, Hákon ordered him killed. Gissur Þorvaldsson arrived at Snorri's farmhouse at Reykholt with seventy men in the middle of the night.

Snorri was found hiding in his cellar. His last recorded words were "Eigi skal höggva." Do not strike.

They struck.

The Icelandic Commonwealth outlived him by barely twenty years. By 1262, the chieftains were exhausted. The bloodshed had gone on too long, the clans were too broken, and Hákon's Norway was offering peace in exchange for submission. They signed the Old Covenant — the Gamli sáttmáli — and handed themselves to the Norwegian king.

It was not conquest. It was surrender by choice, which is somehow more Icelandic. A people who had settled the edge of the world rather than accept a king had spent three centuries building something extraordinary: a parliament, a legal tradition, a literary culture, and a very specific stubbornness about doing things their own way. They handed it over when the alternative was more of their own neighbours killing each other.

What they built in those three and a half centuries didn't disappear. It went underground.

The Althing would be abolished, restored, abolished again. Foreign kings would trade Iceland like a ledger item. Plagues would come. Famines would come. Pirates from North Africa would raid the coast. The country would be occupied by Britain, then America. Its banks would explode. Its volcano would shut down European airspace.

And through all of it, that original instinct — the one that made people sail to the edge of the world in the first place — kept surfacing. The stubbornness. The pragmatism. The willingness to do something nobody else thought was rational and simply refuse to back down.

October 2008. A tiny island. Eleven times its GDP in collapsing bank assets. The IMF at the door.

The foundation for what happened next was laid in 874.
