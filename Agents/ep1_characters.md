# Character Design Specs — Episode 1
## "How to Steal an Island" — Iceland Origins (874 AD – 1262)

---

## NAMED HISTORICAL FIGURES

---

### 1. The Irish Papar Monk

**Name & Role**
Unnamed representative hermit of the Papar — the Irish Christian ascetics who inhabited Iceland before the Norse arrived. He embodies the original instinct that drives the whole episode: choosing remoteness over the world, solitude over society.

**Design**
- Build: Small, slight, wiry — shorter than every other character in the episode
- Distinguishing features: Tonsure haircut (shaved crown, ring of hair), bare feet in simple leather sandals, permanently closed or half-closed eyes suggesting inner calm
- Costume: Rough undyed cream-grey wool habit, belted with a simple rope cord; no ornamentation; a small worn leather satchel hangs from one shoulder
- Props: Holds a small hand-bell in one hand; a slim manuscript book tucked under the other arm; a short wooden crozier (bishop's staff, miniaturised) leaning against him when seated
- Color palette: Undyed cream, warm off-white, muted grey — no saturation; fits the pre-settlement "blank slate" palette

**Expression & Personality**
Default expression: serene contentment — eyes softly closed or lowered, the faintest smile. Before a word is spoken, the viewer reads: this person has already found what they were looking for. The design must communicate peace without smugness.

**Key Scene**
The longship appears on the horizon. His expression transitions from pure serenity to deep, quiet resignation — not fear, not anger, just the weight of someone thinking "of course." He methodically packs his bell, book, and crozier into his bag and walks off screen. He does not look back. The viewer should feel the absurdity and the dignity simultaneously.

**Recurring visual**
The tonsure haircut and the hand-bell. Even in silhouette, the distinctive shaved-crown-ring-of-hair shape and the small bell in the raised hand are immediately recognisable. The bell appears once more as a background detail after he leaves — still standing in the empty shore.

### 2. Ingólfr Arnarson

**Name & Role**
Ingólfr Arnarson — the man credited with founding the first permanent Norse settlement at Reykjavík in 874 AD. He is the episode's founding figure: stubborn, pragmatic, slightly absurd in his commitment to doing things his own way. He matters because the episode's entire thesis — Iceland as a refuge for people who refuse to be governed — begins with him.

**Design**
- Build: Tall, broad-shouldered, weathered — the largest Norse figure on screen except for Harald; built like someone who has sailed for a very long time
- Distinguishing features: A short, well-kept dark beard going grey at the jaw; deep-set eyes with the look of someone who has made a decision and is not revisiting it; a slight forward lean at the shoulders, permanent nautical posture
- Costume: Fur-collared heavy wool cloak in dark brown, leather arm-bracers, practical tunic and breeches in undyed grey-brown; no crown, no elaborate adornment — this is a man who has chosen function; a short-handled axe hangs at his belt (not drawn, just present)
- Props: The two carved high-seat pillars are his signature prop — seen first in his hands, then floating away, then finally standing planted in Reykjavík. He also stands at a ship's prow repeatedly.
- Color palette: Dark brown, burnt umber, warm grey — firmly in the Settlement-era amber palette; the fur collar adds warmth

**Expression & Personality**
Default expression: the particular stubbornness of a man who has made a decision he cannot un-make — jaw set, eyes forward, not quite frowning. Before a word is spoken: committed and slightly inflexible. There is no doubt in his face, which is both his strength and his comedy.

**Key Scene**
He releases the two carved pillars into the sea. His hands open. His expression is resolute. The pillars bob away. He continues to watch. They are still floating. He is still watching. The viewer should feel warm amusement at the absolute seriousness with which he treats his own deeply impractical decision — and quiet respect that it worked.

**Recurring visual**
The fur-collared cloak and forward-leaning posture. In every appearance, the cloak's distinctive fur collar identifies him instantly at small size. He is always either looking at something on the horizon or at the two pillars, which bookend his appearances.

### 3. King Harald Fairhair (Harald Hárfagri)

**Name & Role**
King Harald Fairhair of Norway — the consolidating monarch whose power-grab drives the Settlement Age migration. He is not a villain; he is simply a king doing what kings do, which is the problem. He represents the authority that people sailed across the North Atlantic to escape.

**Design**
- Build: Large, wide, self-satisfied — the widest silhouette in the episode; he takes up space deliberately
- Distinguishing features: Extraordinary hair — long, impressively maintained, slightly ridiculous in its perfection; the name demands this. Clean-shaven face, or a very neat short beard. An expression of mild, permanent self-satisfaction.
- Costume: Rich deep red-and-gold tunic with embroidered trim; an elaborate crown (not comically large, but unmistakably grand); heavy fur-lined outer robe in deep burgundy; multiple arm rings (status markers)
- Props: Nothing in hand — he doesn't need to carry things; other people carry things for him. His crown is his prop.
- Color palette: Deep red, gold, dark burgundy — deliberately contrasting with Iceland's amber palette; his Norway scenes are richer, more opulent, more uncomfortable

**Expression & Personality**
Default expression: mild, comfortable self-satisfaction — the face of someone who considers the current arrangement entirely reasonable. Before a word is spoken: authority that doesn't notice or care about its cost to others. He should not read as cruel — just complacently powerful.

**Key Scene**
He appears in the background of Norway as Ingólfr walks away — large, still, pleased. His "consolidating power" label appears near his impressive hair. He waves slightly toward the departing Ingólfr, as if magnanimously. The viewer should feel: yes, I would also leave.

**Recurring visual**
The extraordinary hair and the elaborate crown. He only appears briefly but his silhouette — wide, crowned, magnificently-haired — is unmistakable. He is always slightly in the background, which is accurate: he causes the migration without ever needing to try.

### 4. Þorgeir Þorkelsson (Lawspeaker)

**Name & Role**
Þorgeir Þorkelsson — the pagan Lawspeaker of the Althing who, around the year 1000, was tasked with deciding whether Iceland would convert to Christianity. He is the episode's most intellectually complex figure: a man who solves an impossible problem with breathtaking pragmatism, at personal cost.

**Design**
- Build: Medium height, lean; older than Ingólfr — perhaps fifty; not frail but worn in a thoughtful way, like a man who has been using his mind rather than his body
- Distinguishing features: Deep lines around the eyes and mouth from years of speaking publicly; hair worn back, going white; a scholar's slightly hunched posture from reading and speaking; eyes that are always slightly calculating, even at rest
- Costume: Plain dark grey wool robe with a simple leather belt — the Lawspeaker's costume is deliberately unadorned, signalling that his authority comes from knowledge, not rank; a heavy fur cloak (the cloak he retreats under) is draped over one shoulder
- Props: The large fur cloak is his defining prop — seen draped over him, then covering him entirely, then falling away. No weapons. No religious symbols on his person.
- Color palette: Muted grey, dark earth tones, off-white — the palette of someone who stands between factions; neither the warm amber of the settlers nor the rich red of Norwegian royalty

**Expression & Personality**
Default expression: intelligent and tired, with the specific look of a man who has been handed something impossible and is not going to pretend otherwise. Before a word is spoken: this person carries weight. Not burdened exactly — they have borne weight before — just: present with what this costs.

**Key Scene**
He sits cross-legged under the fur cloak. The sun moves across the sky. Stars appear. The sun returns. The cloak does not move. Then it does. He stands. His expression has changed: it is settled now, resolved. He has paid whatever price the night cost him and he has an answer. The viewer should feel quiet awe — not religious awe, but the specific respect for someone who did the right thing at personal cost for reasons of pure civic duty.

**Recurring visual**
The fur cloak. Even before he disappears under it, it hangs from his shoulder as a sign of his office. When he emerges from it, the cloak falls open and he stands differently — resolved where he was calculating before.

### 5. Erik the Red (Eiríkr Rauði)

**Name & Role**
Erik the Red — exiled from Norway for manslaughter, exiled from Iceland for manslaughter, sailed west and named a massive ice-covered island "Greenland" specifically to attract settlers. He is the episode's great opportunist: a man who turns his pattern of consequences into an accidental legacy.

**Design**
- Build: Broad and solid, shorter than Ingólfr but denser; built like someone who has survived a great deal of their own decisions
- Distinguishing features: Big red beard — the name demands it; full, unkempt, vivid red-orange; a scar above one eyebrow (earned, not decorative); wide-set eyes with an expression caught between confidence and the specific awareness of a man who has been asked to leave several places
- Costume: Heavy fur vest over a rough linen tunic; practical leather breeches, worn boots; no crown, no rank markers — he has forfeited all of those; a hand-axe on his belt (the instrument of his history)
- Props: His ship's prow (he is seen at it repeatedly); the coastline of Greenland behind him; the two exile documents appearing beside him
- Color palette: Burnt orange (matching his beard), warm earth brown, dark leather — strong contrast with the cool grey-white of the Greenland ice he's standing in front of

**Expression & Personality**
Default expression: confident with a slightly calculating overlay — not calculating like Þorgeir (intellectual), but calculating like a man assessing how a situation can be turned to his advantage. Before a word is spoken: this person has a plan, and the plan involves other people's assumptions. Not malicious — entrepreneurial.

**Key Scene**
He stands on the Greenland coast. The ice is visible behind him. A thought bubble is initially blank. Then it fills with the word "Greenland." His expression shifts to: inspired. Calculated. Committed. The viewer should feel the absurdity of the fraud and the genuine admiration that it worked.

**Recurring visual**
The red beard and the hand-axe at his belt. The beard is his entire identity at small size — vivid red-orange makes him instantly locatable in any frame. He always appears at the prow of his ship or against an icy background, which creates a consistent visual context.

### 6. Leif Eriksson (Leifr Eiríksson)

**Name & Role**
Leif Eriksson — Erik the Red's son, who sailed west from Greenland around the year 1000 and reached North America, establishing a settlement at what is now L'Anse aux Meadows in Newfoundland. He is the episode's great underplayer: a man who did something extraordinary and then simply went home.

**Design**
- Build: Lighter and taller than his father — the same broad shoulders, but less dense, not yet weathered to the same degree; younger by a generation, early thirties
- Distinguishing features: Fair hair where his father had red; a shorter, neater beard; his father's wide-set eyes, but with genuine curiosity in them rather than calculation; a slight lean forward, as if permanently interested in what is over the horizon
- Costume: Similar Norse seafaring kit to his father but newer, better-maintained — this man has resources and a functioning settlement behind him; a good wool cloak in dark blue-grey, leather armour underneath
- Props: His hand shades his eyes as he looks west — this is his defining gesture. The map with Newfoundland and the "Leifsbuðir" label. The empty filing cabinet.
- Color palette: Dark blue-grey, cool stone tones — slightly cooler than the Greenland/Icelandic amber; he's further west, further into the unknown

**Expression & Personality**
Default expression: genuine, light curiosity — not driven like his father, not burdened like Þorgeir; he is simply interested in what is there. Before a word is spoken: this is someone who goes places because the places are interesting, not because they are fleeing. His lightness is the comedy; he finds an entire continent and goes home as if he'd popped out for supplies.

**Key Scene**
His ship turns around and sails back east from Newfoundland. He is standing at the stern, looking back briefly at the coast, then turning to face east again. The empty filing cabinet appears beside the frame. His expression is unbothered — not dismissive, just: done. The viewer should feel a specific comic affection and mild historical exasperation.

**Recurring visual**
The hand-shading-eyes gesture and the dark blue-grey cloak. He appears only twice but these markers keep him immediately recognisable. He is always either looking west or heading east.

### 7. Snorri Sturluson

**Name & Role**
Snorri Sturluson, born 1179 — historian, mythographer, politician. The man who wrote down Norse mythology (the Prose Edda) and is therefore the reason Thor, Odin, and Loki exist in their current form. He is the episode's most complex arc: intellectual brilliance and political ambition converging on a bad end in a cellar. He matters because without him, half of what the modern world calls "Norse" would not exist.

**Design**
- Build: Medium build, scholar's frame — not slight but not physical either; he is a man of rooms and desks, though capable of navigating politics
- Distinguishing features: Sharp, intelligent features; a short dark beard; slight ink staining on the fingers of his right hand (permanent, visible in close shots); he ages across the episode — introduced at perhaps forty (focused, confident), last seen at sixty-two (wary, exposed)
- Costume: When writing: a good wool robe in deep green-brown, ink-stained at the cuffs; when appearing as politician: the same robe but with a fine fur-trimmed cloak added, slightly uncomfortable on him — he is a writer dressed as a lord. Never fully one or the other.
- Props: Quill and manuscript page when at his desk; a lamp when in the cellar (the single most important visual). The desk and stacked manuscripts are a recurring environment, not a prop he carries.
- Color palette: Deep candlelight gold, warm earth green, dark ink-brown — the Saga Age palette; shifts to cooler grey-green as the Sturlung Age arrives

**Expression & Personality**
Default expression (at desk): bright, absorbed, slightly self-aware — a man simultaneously composing something important and aware that he is very good at it. He is not modest. He is not wrong. His second expression (late in the episode): subtly wary, like a man paying attention to sounds outside the room. The transition between these two expressions is his character arc. Before a word is spoken: intelligence and the specific vulnerability of someone who believes that being smart is protection.

**Key Scene**
The cellar. Low ceiling, stone walls. Snorri crouched in the corner, holding the lamp up. He looks up. This is the face of a man who has run out of moves. The viewer should feel the specific grief of watching the person who gave us Norse mythology die because he was also a politician.

**Recurring visual**
The quill in hand (or ink on fingers when the quill is not present) and the deep green-brown robe. In every appearance, the robe identifies him — whether he is at his desk, appearing in court, or crouched in a cellar. The lamp in his hand in the final scene echoes the candlelight of his working environment but inverted: now the light is for hiding, not for writing.

### 8. King Hákon IV Hákonarson (of Norway)

**Name & Role**
King Hákon IV of Norway — the patient, calculating Norwegian monarch who backs different factions during the Sturlung Age and eventually orders the assassination of Snorri Sturluson. He represents the external force that the Icelandic Commonwealth was designed to avoid: a monarch who doesn't need to invade because he can simply wait.

**Design**
- Build: Medium-tall, not imposing physically — his power comes from stillness, not size; lean, precise in his movements; older than Ingólfr was in 874, the age of a man who has been patient for decades
- Distinguishing features: A composed, smooth face that gives nothing away — no visible anger, no visible warmth; close-cropped dark hair, slightly greying at the temples; formal posture, always very still
- Costume: Norwegian royal dress — richer and more formal than any Icelandic character; a fine wool surcoat in deep blue-black over chain mail; an understated crown (smaller than Harald Fairhair's, more expensive-looking); no fur (he is indoors, in a court)
- Props: A chess piece, which he turns over slowly. This is his visual signature. He is always calculating a move, never rushing it.
- Color palette: Deep blue-black, cool silver, dark burgundy — Norway's colors contrast with Iceland's amber; his palette is the coldest in the episode

**Expression & Personality**
Default expression: patient and calculating, but expressed as simple neutrality — he is not brooding, not sneering, just completely still and very attentive. Before a word is spoken: this person is in no hurry, which is itself a form of menace. The design must absolutely not make him look evil — he should look like a reasonable king making reasonable decisions. That is the horror.

**Key Scene**
He is at the right edge of the map, across the sea, watching Iceland's Sturlung Age unfold. He turns a chess piece over slowly in his hand. He is not involved — yet. The camera holds on his face. He is patient. His expression does not change. The viewer should feel the specific dread of watching someone patient, watching and waiting.

**Recurring visual**
The chess piece, the dark blue-black surcoat, the total stillness. Every time Hákon appears, he is still, composed, and the chess piece is present. In his final appearance, the chess piece is placed. That placement is the death of Snorri.

### 9. Gissur Þorvaldsson

**Name & Role**
Gissur Þorvaldsson — the Icelandic chieftain who, on Hákon's orders, arrived at Snorri Sturluson's farmhouse at Reykholt with seventy men and killed him. He is the episode's most morally uncomfortable figure: not a villain, just an instrument. A man doing a job. That is what makes the scene work.

**Design**
- Build: Solid, military bearing, middling height — a man built for enforcement rather than inspiration; functional, unremarkable in the way that instruments are unremarkable
- Distinguishing features: A plain, unremarkable face — this is intentional and important; no scar, no sneer, no memorable feature; close-cropped hair, a short beard; the expression of someone who is working
- Costume: Practical dark-grey wool travel cloak over chain mail and leather; a sword at his belt; no decoration — he is not here for ceremony; the cloak is damp (it is a night raid)
- Props: His presence with seventy men is the prop — he does not need to carry anything remarkable; the seventy figures behind him are part of his visual
- Color palette: Dark grey, black, cold night tones — this scene has no warm amber; the palette has fully shifted to the cold dark of the Sturlung Age

**Expression & Personality**
Default expression: professional. Not gleeful, not reluctant, not conflicted — just doing a job. This is the most important design decision in the episode. Before a word is spoken: this man would look the same arriving at a trade negotiation. The horror is in the normalcy.

**Key Scene**
He leads seventy small figures across a dark field toward a farmhouse with one lit window. His expression is not triumphant. He arrives and enters. He is not the point. He is the fact. The viewer should feel: this is how institutions kill — through ordinary people performing ordinary functions.

**Recurring visual**
He appears only in the single night scene. His visual signature is the damp dark cloak and the unremarkable face — recognisable as "not recognisable," which is its own kind of marker. He is the last human figure before the episode's emotional pivot.

## ARCHETYPE CHARACTERS

---

### A. The Goðar — Althing Chieftains (Archetype)

**Note:** Not a specific person. Represents the thirty-six chieftains who founded and populated the Althing in 930. They represent the distributed power structure of the Icelandic Commonwealth — not democracy, but deliberative oligarchy with a functional system of public law.

**Design**
- Each of the thirty-six has a distinct silhouette — varying heights, builds, beard styles, and cloak colors — but they share design DNA: Norse era clothing, practical wool cloaks in varied muted tones (grey, brown, dark green, rust), arm rings, no crowns
- The variety of their silhouettes is the point: no single figure dominates; the visual message is distributed power
- They are shown twice: first filing into Þingvellir in 930, deliberate and purposeful; second in 1262, fewer in number, thinner, standing in a ragged exhausted group signing the Old Covenant
- Color palette: Varied — warm amber and earth tones in 930, progressively greyer and darker by 1262

**Expression & Personality**
In 930: purposeful, engaged, talking. In 1262: exhausted and resigned — the same deliberative posture collapsed by forty years of civil war.

**Key Scene**
The contrast between their first appearance (filing in, sky open above them, full thirty-six in deliberate semi-circle) and their last (ragged group, fewer, marking the Old Covenant document) is the episode's visual thesis: the Commonwealth built itself and then destroyed itself.

**Recurring visual**
The semi-circle formation. Even depleted in 1262, the remnant chieftains unconsciously form a fragment of the same shape. The viewer who remembers the 930 scene will feel the loss without narration.

---

### B. The Lawspeaker (Generic — Althing scene)

**Note:** Not Þorgeir specifically (he has his own entry above), but the generic Lawspeaker visual in the Althing scene: a figure standing on the Law Rock (Lögberg), arms slightly raised, speaking to the assembled chieftains. This figure represents the oral legal tradition — laws spoken from memory, publicly, as the binding force of a stateless society.

**Design**
- Similar visual language to Þorgeir: plain dark robe, no weapons, elevated position; arms raised in the posture of speech
- Distinguished from Þorgeir by context (he is at the Althing in the establishment scene, not in the conversion crisis)
- Color palette: Same muted grey-brown as Þorgeir's general design — the Lawspeaker is always visually neutral

**Appearances:** One scene — Part Four, Althing establishment.

---

### C. The Settler Figures (Migration Archetype)

**Note:** Not specific individuals. Represent the 8,000–20,000 settlers who arrived in Iceland between 874 and 930. They appear as a population-level visual — small figures filling the Iceland map, arriving from two directions (Norway to the right, British Isles below).

**Design**
- Small, simplified silhouettes — not individually detailed; must read as people at map scale
- Varied heights, small children visible among the adults; a few figures carry bundles, crates, small livestock
- A subset include visibly darker-complected figures from the British Isles (accurate to the genetic evidence of Irish/Scottish DNA)
- Color palette: Warm amber — they are the content of the Settlement Age; their collective presence is what turns the empty map warm

**Appearances:** One major scene (Part Three, Great Migration); briefly in the Cold Open montage echo.

---

### D. King Óláfr Tryggvason (Background Archetype)

**Note:** Not Hákon and not Harald — this is Óláfr, the Norwegian king pressuring Iceland's conversion around the year 1000. He appears only once, briefly, in the background of the conversion crisis scene. He is included as a distinct visual because his pressure is the cause of Þorgeir's impossible task.

**Design**
- Similar design DNA to Harald Fairhair but a generation later and less self-satisfied — he is a convert himself, and his expression is the earnest conviction of the recently converted
- Norwegian royal dress, but slightly simpler than Harald's opulence; his dominant color is the same deep red as Norway's palette
- He appears small in the background, one hand extended toward Iceland — the gesture reads as both invitation and demand

**Appearances:** One scene — Part Five, Year 1000 conversion.
