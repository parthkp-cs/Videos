# Agent Prompt: Audio Script
## Step 2 of 5 — Clean Narration Script

**Variable:** `{COUNTRY}`
**Input:** Research dossier from Step 1

---

## Instructions for agent

Using the research dossier provided, write a complete audio-only narration script covering the entire history of **{COUNTRY}** from earliest origins to the present day.

### Format rules
- Audio only. No stage directions, no visual cues, no headers, no brackets.
- Pure narration that reads naturally when spoken aloud.
- Natural paragraph breaks for breath and pacing.
- Target: **1,400–1,600 words** (approximately 10–12 minutes of pure narration; with visual holds the total video lands at 14–18 minutes).
- **Split the script into 3 parts** at natural narrative boundaries — mark each split with `--- PART 2 BEGINS ---` and `--- PART 3 BEGINS ---`. These become separate audio files for production. Split points should fall at the end of a completed narrative arc, never mid-sentence or mid-thought.

### Tone rules (strictly follow these)

The voice is a smart, well-read friend who just came back from a rabbit hole about {COUNTRY} and absolutely cannot shut up about it. Casual. Conversational. Like they're telling you this over a drink, not presenting a paper.

**Tone breakdown to hit precisely:**
- **100% factually accurate.** Every claim sourced from the research dossier. No embellishment of facts.
- **~10% sarcastic.** Dry, deadpan observations about situations, decisions, or ironies — maybe one every 2–3 paragraphs. Never directed at people or cultures. Think: raised eyebrow at a historical irony, not mockery.
- **~20% punny.** Wordplay woven naturally throughout — roughly one pun or clever turn of phrase every 4–5 sentences. Never forced. If a pun needs a runway, it doesn't belong.
- **~20% fun.** Energy and delight in the telling. Surprising facts delivered with genuine enthusiasm. The listener should feel the narrator is having a great time with this material.

**What these look like in practice:**
- Sarcasm (10%): "He named the mostly ice-covered island Greenland. For the marketing. The marketing worked." One line. Deadpan. Move on.
- Punny (20%): wordplay on place names, titles, situations — natural, not laboured.
- Fun (20%): "His son Leif sailed west and found an entire continent. Told a few people. Then went home." Short punchy beats that make the listener smile at the audacity of history.

**What to avoid:**
- Sarcasm at people or cultures — always at situations and decisions only
- Puns that need explaining
- Jokes that require the country or its people to be the punchline
- Straining for laughs — if a line doesn't land naturally, cut it and say the fact straight
- Filler phrases ("Throughout history...", "As we can see...", "In conclusion...")
- Starting with geography unless geography IS the story
- Ending on a low note

### Structure rules
1. **Cold open** — Start at the most dramatic or surprising modern moment from the research dossier. Hook the listener. Make them ask "how did we get here?" Do NOT start at the beginning of history.
2. **Rewind** — A single line that explicitly rewinds the story to the very beginning.
3. **Chronological history** — Tell the full story from earliest origins forward.
4. **Close the loop** — Return to the cold open moment as the payoff. Show why the full history explains that opening moment.
5. **Modern close** — End on something that makes the listener feel the country is genuinely remarkable.

### 3-part split guidelines
- **Part 1** ends after the founding era is complete — parliament/early government established, before religious or expansionist turning points.
- **Part 2** ends after the literary/cultural golden age or equivalent peak, before the collapse or civil war.
- **Part 3** covers collapse through to the modern close.
- Each part should be roughly equal in length (±20%). Do not create a very short Part 3.

### Accuracy rules
Every fact in the script must come directly from the research dossier. Do not introduce new claims, new statistics, or new historical details that were not in the research. If the dossier flagged something as uncertain, do not include it as a fact — either omit it or frame it explicitly as disputed or legendary.
